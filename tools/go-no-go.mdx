Not every published tender is genuinely open to competition. When a public buyer wants to renew with their incumbent supplier, the specification — whether intentionally or not — is sometimes drafted in ways that make it almost impossible for anyone else to win. Spotting these signals before you invest days in a technical response is one of the highest-leverage decisions a B2G sales team can make. Skult's Go/No-Go Analysis tool runs an AI evaluation of your uploaded CCTP (technical specification) or RC (consultation rules) against seven favoritism indicators, and delivers a clear recommendation in under a minute — all inside your browser, so the document never leaves your device.

## The seven favoritism indicators

The AI checks every uploaded document against these seven signals, each of which — individually or in combination — can indicate that a tender has been written with a specific incumbent in mind:

| # | Indicator | What it looks like |
|---|-----------|-------------------|
| 1 | **Custom-fit technical requirements** | Specifications that describe a very specific configuration, methodology, or capability profile that only one supplier realistically matches |
| 2 | **Disguised product or brand references** | Requirements that effectively name a proprietary product, platform, or technology without using its brand name directly |
| 3 | **Rare or hard-to-obtain certifications** | Mandatory qualifications held by very few suppliers, particularly if they are niche or recently introduced |
| 4 | **Unrealistically short response deadlines** | A submission window so brief that only a supplier already familiar with the dossier (i.e. the incumbent) can realistically prepare a compliant response |
| 5 | **Imbalanced scoring criteria** | Award criteria weighted heavily toward attributes where the current supplier has a structural advantage — such as past experience on this specific contract or this specific buyer |
| 6 | **Overly narrow scope definition** | A contract scope described so precisely that it mirrors the incumbent's current service delivery model rather than defining the buyer's actual need |
| 7 | **Experience-based prequalification** | Eligibility conditions that require prior experience on contracts of a specific size, sector, or configuration that only a long-standing incumbent would have |

<Note>
  No single indicator is automatically disqualifying. The AI evaluates them in combination and in context. A No-Go recommendation signals that multiple indicators are present and together suggest the tender is not genuinely open — but the final decision always rests with you.
</Note>

## Frequently asked questions

<Accordion title="Is the analysis really free?">
  Yes, completely free — no account, no credit card, and no usage limits. You can analyse as many tender documents as you like.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Which document types can I analyse?">
  The tool accepts PDF, DOCX, and TXT files up to a maximum of 10 MB. These cover the vast majority of CCTP and RC documents issued by French public buyers. If your document is larger than 10 MB, try extracting the relevant sections into a new file before uploading.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Are my documents confidential?">
  Yes. The analysis runs entirely inside your browser. Your document is never sent to a server, never stored, and never accessible to anyone other than you during the current session. Once you close or refresh the page, nothing is retained.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="What is a favoritism indicator?">
  A favoritism indicator is a signal in a tender document that suggests the specification may have been written to favour a specific supplier — typically the incumbent. Each indicator represents a pattern that, alone or in combination with others, makes it statistically harder for a new entrant to win. The seven indicators used by Skult's tool are based on patterns commonly identified in public procurement audits and case law in France.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="How reliable is the Go/No-Go recommendation?">
  The recommendation reflects the AI's reading of the document against the seven defined indicators. It is designed to flag risk, not to replace your commercial judgement. A No-Go recommendation means the document shows multiple signs of a potentially closed tender — but there may be context the AI cannot assess (e.g. a strong pre-existing relationship with the buyer, a genuinely unique product fit). Treat the output as a structured first filter, not a final verdict.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Does this replace a proper tender analysis?">
  No — it complements it. The Go/No-Go tool is designed to be a fast first filter that takes under a minute, helping you decide whether a tender is worth the hours of detailed analysis that a full technical response requires. If the tool returns a Go, you should still conduct a thorough review of the document before committing to a response.
</Accordion>
