The Contract Radar surfaces public contracts that are approaching expiry — before anyone has issued a tender notice. Because most French public contracts run for 1 to 4 years, their renewal is predictable: the buyer opens an internal reflection window roughly 6 to 9 months before the contract ends. The Radar lets you step into that window first, giving you a genuine opportunity to influence the specification before it's written. It draws from the DECP dataset (~940,000 contracts) and calculates estimated expiry dates from the notification date plus the declared contract duration.

## How to use it

<Steps>
  <Step title="Open the Radar">
    Navigate to [outils.skult.ai/radar-marches](https://outils.skult.ai/radar-marches). No login is required.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Enter a keyword, CPV code, or department">
    In the search field, type a free-text keyword describing the type of contract you're interested in (e.g. *maintenance informatique*, *nettoyage*, *espaces verts*). You can also enter a CPV code directly if you know it, or type a department number (e.g. *31* for Haute-Garonne) to narrow results geographically. Combine filters for a more focused result set.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Select your time horizon">
    Choose whether you want to see contracts expiring within **6 months**, **9 months**, or **12 months**. The Radar calculates estimated expiry dates by adding each contract's declared duration to its notification date, then filters to show only those falling within your chosen window.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Review the results">
    The results list shows the buyer name, contract type, awarded amount (HT), and the estimated expiry month. A volume chart above the list displays how many contracts expire in each upcoming month — useful for spotting seasonal peaks in your sector.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Open a contract record">
    Click any result to view the full contract details: the current awardee, the CPV sector code, the original notification date, and the full contract object description. This tells you who currently holds the contract and roughly how long they've been the incumbent.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Plan your outreach">
    Use the buyer's name and the expiry date to time your approach. Aim to make first contact **3 to 6 months before expiry** — early enough to be considered in the specification phase, but not so far out that the renewal isn't yet a priority for the buyer.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Strategic use of expiry data

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Enter the decision window early" icon="calendar-clock">
    Buyers typically begin reflecting on renewal requirements 6–9 months before a contract expires. Reaching out at this stage means you can have a genuine conversation about needs — not just respond to a specification already tailored to the incumbent.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Displace an entrenched competitor" icon="chess-knight">
    When a competitor has held a contract for several years, renewal is their most vulnerable moment. Use the Radar to identify those accounts and build your case before they lock in another term.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Build a rolling pipeline" icon="arrow-rotate-right">
    Set a regular cadence — run a Radar search each month for your keyword and department — to keep a fresh pipeline of pre-tender opportunities at different stages of maturity.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Prioritise by contract size" icon="arrow-up-wide-short">
    The results include awarded amounts, letting you filter your attention toward higher-value contracts where the commercial effort is most justified.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

<Tip>
  The sweet spot for first contact is **3 to 6 months before the estimated expiry date**. Too early and the renewal isn't yet on the buyer's agenda. Too late and the specification may already be written. A contact 4–5 months out gives you time to run multiple touchpoints before the tender drops.
</Tip>

<Info>
  Expiry dates shown in the Radar are **estimates** calculated from the notification date and declared contract duration in the DECP dataset. Actual expiry may differ if the contract was extended or modified. Always confirm dates during your discovery conversation with the buyer.
</Info>

## Frequently asked questions

<Accordion title="Where does the Radar's data come from?">
  The Contract Radar draws from the DECP (Données Essentielles de la Commande Publique), the official open dataset published on data.gouv.fr. It covers approximately 940,000 public contracts across all categories of French public buyers. Expiry dates are calculated by the tool — they are not published directly in the DECP — by adding the declared contract duration to the notification date.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="How is the expiry date calculated?">
  Each DECP record includes a notification date and a declared contract duration (in months). The Radar adds these two values to estimate the expiry date. This is an approximation: if the original contract was extended, modified, or terminated early, the actual end date may differ.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Can I filter by department?">
  Yes. Enter a department number (e.g. *69* for Rhône, *75* for Paris) in the search field alongside your keyword. You can also leave the department blank to search across all of France.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Is the tool free and unlimited?">
  Yes. The Contract Radar is completely free, with no account required and no limit on the number of searches. Access it at [outils.skult.ai/radar-marches](https://outils.skult.ai/radar-marches).
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="How is this different from watching BOAMP or PLACE?">
  BOAMP and PLACE publish tender notices *after* the buyer has already drafted the specification. By that point, the incumbent typically has a significant advantage. The Contract Radar identifies contracts that are **approaching expiry** — before a notice is published — so you can engage the buyer during the specification phase rather than simply competing on a level already tilted against you.
</Accordion>
